The Mountain Gorilla

Critically endangered - Population 880.

As their name implies, Mountain gorillas live in forests high in the mountains, at elevations of 8,000 to 13,000 feet. Factors contributing to endangerment include:

Poaching - Gorillas are often maimed of killed by traps set for other animals.

Abduction - Infants are abducted for illegal selling to zoos and as pets. This often results in other adult gorillas being killed in the process.

Habitat loss - Rapidly expanding human settlements are removing the gorilla habitat.

The Tapanuli Orangutan

Critically endangered - Population less than 800.

A new species of orangutan was announved in November, 2017. With no more than 800 individuals, the Tapanuli orangutan is the most endangered of all great apes. Factors contributing to its population decline include:

Conflict with humans and the illegal wildlife trade.

Rampant habitat destruction for small-scale agriculture, mining and a proposed hydroelectric dam.

Inbreeding depression due to the small population size.

The Sumatran Elephant

Critically endangered - With a population of 2400.

In 2012, the Sumatran elephant was changed from "Endangered" to "Critically Endangered" because half of its population has been lost in one generation. Here are some factors contributing to population decline:

Conversion of forests into human settlements and agricultural areas. Between 1980 and 2005, 69% of potential Sumatran elephant habitat was lost within just one elephant generation.

Elephants are also targets of poaching for their ivory. Between 1985 and 2007, 50% of Sumatran elephants died.

Between 2012 and 2015, 36 elephants were found dead in Aceh Province due to poisoning, electrocution and traps.

The Sumatran Rhino

Critically endangered - Population of 50.

The Sumatran rhinoceros, also known as the hairy rhinoceros is the only Asian two-horned rhinoceros. It is classed as critically endangered with fewer than 100 individuals:

From the early 1990s, the population decline was estimated at more than 50% per decade, and the small, scattered populations now face high risks of inbreeding depression

Most remaining habitat is in relatively inaccessible mountainous areas of Indonesia

Poaching of Sumatran rhinoceros is a cause for concern, as the price of its horm has been estimated as high as US$30,000 per kilogram.

The Sumatran Tiger

Endangered - Population less than 1000.

The Sumatran tiger lives in the Indonesian island of Sumatra. Its population was listed as Critically Endangered on the IUCN Red List in 2008, as it was estimated at 441 to 679 individuals. Major threats include:

Despite being given full protection in Indonesia and internationally, tiger parts are still found openly in trade in SUmatra. Farmers are probably the main hunters of tigers in Sumatra.

Kerinci Seblat National Park, which has the largest recorded population of tigers, is suffering a high rate of deforestation in its outer regions.
